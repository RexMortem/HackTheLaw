## 1. Background

In complex litigation, legal teams need to move from a large body of documents to a clear case theory. Pleadings may contain dozens of allegations, denials and legal elements that need to be proved, but the supporting evidence is often scattered across witness statements, correspondence, expert materials, contracts and internal records.

At present, lawyers often have to review these materials manually to work out whether each pleaded allegation is supported, contradicted or unsupported by the available evidence. That process is time-consuming and can make it difficult to spot weak points in the case early. The risk is that a legal team may only discover late in the process that an important proposition is not properly evidenced, or that there is contrary evidence that has not been fully accounted for.

## 2. Challenge

Build an AI tool that takes a litigation bundle and stress-tests the case theory by mapping pleaded allegations and defence points to the available evidence. The tool should identify whether each proposition is supported, undermined or left unaddressed by the Pleadings. It should also highlight evidential gaps, contradictions and propositions that may need further witness, expert or documentary support. The aim is not simply to show “who said what” on a topic, but to help a litigation team understand whether its pleaded case can actually be proved on the evidence available.

## 3. Scenario

Participants are acting for a litigation team in a fictional commercial dispute. The team will be provided access to a short dispute bundle, which may include pleadings, witness statements, expert extracts, correspondence, contractual documents and internal records.

The pleadings set out a number of allegations and denials. Some are well supported by the documents, some are contradicted by parts of the evidence, and some have little or no supporting material. The legal team wants to know which parts of the case are strong, which parts are vulnerable, and where further evidence is needed before trial or settlement discussions.

Participants should build a tool that helps the team test the strength of the case by linking each pleaded proposition to the relevant underlying materials.

## 5. Example Solution(s)

- An example solution could be a pleading-to-proof matrix that extracts each pleaded allegation or defence point and categorises the available evidence as supportive, adverse, neutral or missing.
- Another solution could be a case theory risk dashboard that ranks propositions by litigation risk, showing where the case is well evidenced and where it may be vulnerable.
- Participants could also build a gap analysis report that identifies propositions requiring further evidence, such as an additional witness statement, expert clarification or document request.
- A more advanced solution could include source-linked citations, confidence scoring, contradiction detection and a “trial readiness” summary that explains which parts of the case theory are strongest and weakest.

## 6. Impact

This solution would help litigation teams move faster from document review to strategic case assessment. It would allow lawyers to identify evidential strengths and weaknesses earlier, prioritise further work more effectively, and prepare for settlement, mediation or trial with a clearer view of the proof available.

For clients, the solution could improve the quality of legal advice by making case-risk analysis more transparent and evidence-based. It could also reduce time spent on manual cross-referencing and help teams focus their attention on the issues that matter most.
