> Main issue: with AI taking over argument formation, we risk hindering the flourishing of jurisprudence and case law as it only thinks backwards and draws on what has previously been decided, instead of encouraging new or unconventional approaches.
> 

solution: 

1. An AI tool whose focus is to allow lawyers to use AI to not just source dicta and precedent but actually take unconventional pathways.

key idea:

1. Build a very classic research AI that extracts important case info. AI extracts every pleaded allegation and defence point. Maps each one to the available evidence. Outputs: supported / undermined / gap. This is table stakes — every team will do some version of this. You need it, but it's not your differentiator.
2. Add a feature called the **unconventional arguments engine (our differentiator)**
- Searches for **obiter dicta** from relevant cases that weren't binding but gestured toward a legal principle that could be argued here. Obiter is gold for unconventional arguments precisely because it's been said by judges but never tested as a ratio.
- Pulls **academic literature** that have proposed arguments or frameworks not yet adopted in case law. Essentially finding the academics who were ahead of the courts.
- Checks **cross-border jurisprudence** — how have courts in other common law jurisdictions (Canada, Australia, Singapore, South Africa) decided similar questions? Especially useful post-Brexit where EU law is no longer binding but can still be persuasive.
- For EU law specifically (where CELLAR becomes invaluable) — what do EU directives and CJEU decisions say that could be argued as persuasive in a UK context?

Issue: During AI training in the US/China/Elsewhere, AI develops internal biases which are not visible to the “naked eye” but long-term can have devastating impacts if left unchallenged. 

The idea: the AI that analyses your case has biases baked into it: trained predominantly on English-language, common law, Western legal sources. When it assesses whether a proposition is "well supported" it's making judgment calls influenced by that training.

Your tool surfaces this explicitly:

- Flags when an AI confidence score might be skewed because the legal question has been litigated more in one jurisdiction than another
- Shows the *distribution* of sources the AI drew on — "this assessment is based 80% on English case law, 15% US, 5% other — here's how the picture changes if you weight non-English sources more heavily"
- Highlights where academic literature *disagrees* with the prevailing case law — giving the lawyer the option to mount a challenge to orthodoxy rather than accept it

This reframes AI bias from a problem to a feature:  you're not hiding it, you're making it legible and useful.
